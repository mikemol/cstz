\section{Worked example: $\partial \circ \partial = 0$ on $\bigwedge(\GF(2)^3)$}
\label{app:examples}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

This appendix instantiates the planner's machinery
on one concrete workload: verifying the
boundary-squared identity at $n = 3$ over all
exterior elements. In the CSTZ codebase this is
\texttt{cstz.verification.check\_boundary\_squared\_all(3)}
(\texttt{src/cstz/verification.py:32}); in [DAF]
it is Proposition 5.5.

\subsection{The workload}

For each of the $2^8 = 256$ exterior elements
$f \in \bigwedge(\GF(2)^3)$, compute $\partial
(\partial f)$ and assert it equals zero. The DAG
has 256 independent instances of the atom sequence:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,label=(\arabic*)]
  \item $\partial_{\mathrm{gather}}$ at $n = 3$:
    input $f$ (8 bits), output $\partial f$ (8
    bits).
  \item $\partial_{\mathrm{gather}}$ at $n = 3$:
    input $\partial f$, output $\partial^2 f$
    (8 bits).
  \item $\mathrm{is\_zero}$: input $\partial^2 f$,
    output a single bit (1 iff all 8 coefficients
    are 0).
  \item $\mathrm{assert}$: fail the workload if the
    bit is 0.
\end{enumerate}

All 256 instances are independent; the natural
tile size of $\partial_{\mathrm{gather}}$ is
$d = 3$ (operates on 8-bit vectors indexed by
3-bit subsets).

\subsection{Hardware profile}

For illustration, assume a hardware profile with
\[
  M = 16,
  \quad B_{\VRAM} = 10^9 \text{ B},
  \quad B_{\SMEM} = 10^5 \text{ B/SM},
  \quad B_{\REG} = 10^3 \text{ words/thread}.
\]
(These numbers are fabricated for illustration; a
real profile is calibrated per Appendix
\ref{app:kernels}.)

\subsection{Chart build-up}

\emph{Scanner.} The parser reads the workload's
256 instances in turn. For each, it creates a
chart item for the 4-atom sequence, carrying
attributes $(E = \Lambda, r = \text{full}, d = 3,
k = 1, \bar{c} = \pi_0(\partial))$.

\emph{Linearity fusion.} $\partial$ is
$\GF(2)$-linear. The grammar's
linearity-fusion production combines the 256
instances into one batched $\partial$ dispatch
with $k = 256$. Cost combiner for the batched
$\oplus$-style production: \VRAM\ sums (each
instance's input), \LAT\ is amortized.

\emph{Iterated $\partial$.} The second
$\partial$ applies to each of the 256 outputs of
the first. Same fusion: one batched dispatch,
$k = 256$.

\emph{Encoding choice.} The grammar offers two
encodings:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \emph{Plan A} ($\Lambda$-dense): 256 input
    elements, each 8 bits. Per-atom cost:
    $\partial$ as gather-XOR.
  \item \emph{Plan B} (RM-packed): first encode
    to $\zeta(f)$ ($n = 3$ butterfly), apply
    $\partial$ as a linear operator in the RM basis
    (which drops the grade budget by one per
    application), then decode. Overhead: 2 encode
    + 2 decode butterflies.
\end{itemize}

Plan B's grade drop is load-bearing: after two
$\partial$ applications, the grade budget is
$r - 2$, which at $r = 3$ is $r = 1$, a
much-compressed representation. The
\emph{is\_zero} check, however, requires the full
output, so Plan B must decode before the final
check, erasing the grade-compression advantage.
The cost-bounded completer extracts both plans as
Pareto-nondominated.

\subsection{Pareto frontier}

At the workload's output nonterminal, chart cell
$\chi[0, 256]$, the completer finds:

\begin{center}
\scriptsize
\begin{tabular}{l|cccc|c}
\textbf{Plan} & \VRAM & \SMEM & \REG & \LAT &
  \textbf{Dual prices} \\
\hline
A ($\Lambda$-dense) & 4 kB & 2 kB & 32 w & 120 & - \\
B (RM) & 4 kB & 4 kB & 48 w & 200 & - \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

(Units and numbers are illustrative.)

Both plans satisfy the budget. Plan A dominates
Plan B componentwise: the Pareto frontier is
$\{A\}$, and Plan B is pruned.

\subsection{Alternative: the grade-truncated
variant}

If the workload's output nonterminal is not
\texttt{is\_zero} but \texttt{grade\_restrict(1)}
(take the grade-1 component of $\partial^2 f$),
then Plan B would retain the compressed RM form
through the grade restriction:

\emph{Plan B$'$} cost: 4 kB VRAM, 3 kB SMEM,
32 w REG, 150 LAT.

Plan B$'$ now \emph{does not} dominate Plan A and
vice versa (A has lower \LAT, B$'$ has lower
\SMEM). Both are in the Pareto frontier; the
portfolio output is
$\mathcal{P} = \{A, B'\}$.

\subsection{Portfolio packing}

At $d = 3, M = 16$: $L = \lcm(3, 16) = 48$.
Minimum batch coarseness: $(48/3)^2 = 256$.

The workload has exactly 256 instances.
Coincidence: 256 is the minimum coarseness, so
the LCM-packed dispatch has zero spare lanes. No
ambiguous-parse packing is possible for this
workload at this batch size.

If the workload were 128 instances instead, 128
spare lanes would be available. The planner could
pack Plan A on lanes 0--127 and Plan B$'$ on lanes
128--255, giving a concurrent TMR-style
portfolio (one plan per half of the warp). The
extraction rule would be \textsc{First} (the
answer is the same from both plans, assuming both
pass).

\subsection{Dual prices as diagnostic}

At the optimum of Plan A, the binding resource is
(illustratively) \LAT. The dual prices:
\begin{align*}
  \lambda_{\VRAM}^* &= 0 \quad \text{(slack; not binding)} \\
  \lambda_{\SMEM}^* &= 0 \quad \text{(slack; not binding)} \\
  \lambda_{\REG}^*  &= 0 \quad \text{(slack; not binding)}
\end{align*}
All budgets have slack; the objective \LAT\ is
limited only by the atoms' intrinsic costs. The
diagnostic message to the user would be: ``Budget
is not binding; reducing latency requires
algorithm changes or hardware changes, not budget
changes.''

At a smaller budget --- say $B_{\REG} = 30$ words
--- Plan A would violate. The planner would
fallback to a time-shared variant (Plan C) with
\REG\ = 16, \LAT\ = 240. Dual price $\lambda_{\REG}^*$
becomes positive, signaling that relaxing the
register budget would most improve latency at
this configuration.

\subsection{Summary}

The example exercises every grammar feature
discussed in the paper:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Linearity fusion (batched $\partial$).
  \item Encoding choice ($\Lambda$ vs RM).
  \item Pareto dominance pruning.
  \item Portfolio extraction at spare-lane
    batches.
  \item Dual-price diagnostics.
  \item \texttt{time\_share\_register} fallback
    under \REG-binding budget.
\end{itemize}

It does not exercise \texttt{fused\_matrix\_apply}
with heterogeneous atoms, because the workload's
atoms are homogeneous. A second worked example
exercising heterogeneous fusion (e.g.\ a Fano
line check batched alongside several perspective
rotations) is deferred to follow-up work.
